The Architecture of Meaning

Naming is not a creative exercise in brainstorming. It is a rigorous process of strategic reduction, linguistic engineering, and market positioning.

Philosophy

A brand name is the single piece of language that your company will use most frequently. It is the vessel into which all brand equity will eventually be poured.

Because of this, we cannot rely on subjectivity or "gut feelings" when choosing a name. We must evaluate names based on their structural integrity: phonetics, semantics, morphology, and memorability.

I build names that are designed to do a specific job, in a specific market, for a specific audience.

Tools of the Trade

Etymology Databases

Deep research into the historical roots and evolution of words to find hidden layers of meaning.

Phonetic Testing

Analyzing the mouthfeel and sound symbolism of syllables (e.g., bouba/kiki effect).

Trademark Screening

Preliminary knockout searches to ensure viability before involving legal counsel.

The Process

A Structured Approach

1. Discovery & Diagnosis

We begin by mapping the terrain. This involves deep stakeholder interviews to uncover the brand's true DNA. We define the target audience, analyze competitor nomenclature, and identify whitespace in the market.

Output: The Naming Brief — a strategic document that defines the exact parameters and objectives for the new name.

2. Linguistic Exploration

With the brief approved, I begin the lateral exploration. I deconstruct core concepts into root words across Latin, Greek, and Proto-Indo-European origins. I explore morphemes, metaphors, and phonetic structures that evoke the desired brand traits.

Hundreds of raw concepts are generated in this phase before ruthless editing begins.

3. Concept Testing

The long list is filtered through multiple rigorous screens. We check for basic trademark availability using the USPTO database. We run phonetic friction tests to ensure the name is easy to pronounce globally. We test for negative semantic associations in major languages.

Only the top 1% of names survive this gauntlet.

4. Delivery & Rationale

The surviving candidates are presented in a comprehensive presentation. Each name is accompanied by a deep linguistic rationale explaining exactly why it works, how it maps back to the brief, and context mockups showing the name living in the real world.

What Makes a Great Name

  • Distinctive: It breaks category conventions.
  • Evocative: It hints at meaning without describing literally.
  • Euphonic: It sounds pleasant and is easy to say.
  • Brief: It is economical in its use of letters and syllables.
  • Defensible: It is legally protectable as a trademark.
